# Assessment Rubric

The lens the optimizer runs over any subject before it recommends anything. Works on a build, a doc, a strategy, code, a trade thesis, a workflow, a venture. Score each dimension, then let the scores drive the ranked optimizations. You do not have to show every score to the operator; show the ones that explain your top moves.

## The seven dimensions

### 1. Goal sharpness
Is the objective specific and measurable, or is it a vibe? "Grow the product thing" is a vibe. "Sell 150 units in 90 days at 76 percent contribution" is a goal. A fuzzy goal is almost always the highest-leverage fix because everything downstream inherits its fuzziness. If the goal is unclear, sharpening it IS the one move.

Score 1 (vibe) to 5 (specific, measurable, time-bound).

### 2. Real state vs talked state
Separate what actually exists or was decided from what was only discussed. In a chat full of ideas, the talked state is large and the real state is small. The delta is where work hides. Name what is genuinely done, what is half-done, and what is still just talk.

Score 1 (all talk) to 5 (state matches the conversation's claims).

### 3. Leverage (the 80/20)
Find the single move that unlocks the most. Often it is a constraint upstream of everything else: the missing number, the undecided fork, the one broken step in the chain. Ask "if only one thing changed here, what change moves the most?" That is the one move in the output.

Score 1 (no clear leverage point) to 5 (obvious high-leverage move available).

### 4. Risks, gaps, contradictions
What is fragile, unvalidated, missing, or self-contradicting? Look for: numbers asserted but never checked, plans that depend on an unconfirmed input, two parts of the plan that contradict, single points of failure, and claims with no evidence. Cite the specific line or file.

Score 1 (riddled) to 5 (clean, well-validated).

### 5. Fit to the operator's constraints
An optimal move that ignores the operator's reality is not optimal. Check against:
- Time: the operator may work a fixed shift with limited free hours. A move that needs 10 focused hours this week probably loses to one that needs 1.
- Attention: fewer concurrent threads beats more. A move that adds another open loop is suspect.
- Money: budget caps are real. Flag anything that needs capital the operator has not allocated.
- The no-thrash rule: no new venture or experiment when 3+ are already active without killing one first.
- Tier and safety: anything with real-world blast radius (send, post, spend, deploy) names the tier and approval it needs.
- Protected time: respect any windows the operator has reserved as off-limits.

Score 1 (ignores the operator's reality) to 5 (fits time, attention, money, rules).

### 6. Sequencing
Right thing, wrong order is still wrong. Sort the moves into now, next, and not-yet. A move that cannot start until an upstream input exists is not a "now" move no matter how valuable. Surface the true critical path.

Score 1 (no order, everything at once) to 5 (clear now/next/later).

### 7. Cost of inaction
What does waiting cost? Some things decay (a stale decision, a time-boxed opportunity, a deadline). Some do not. This dimension decides how hard to push. If inaction is cheap, a calm recommendation is fine. If a window is closing, say so loudly.

Score 1 (urgent, decaying) to 5 (no rush, safe to sit).

## Turning scores into the output

- The lowest-scoring dimension that is also upstream of the others is usually the one move.
- A goal at 1 or 2 outranks almost everything else; fix the goal first.
- Real state far below talked state means the honest next step is "do the thing," not "plan more."
- Many risks at low scores with high cost-of-inaction means lead with risk mitigation, not growth.
- High fit-to-constraints violations turn an otherwise-good move into a "cut or defer."

## De-optimization checklist (the cut/stop list)

A common failure mode is doing too much at once. Actively look for things to remove:
- Threads with no clear owner or next action that are just consuming attention.
- A fifth concurrent initiative when four are already open.
- Gold-plating: polishing something already good enough while something broken waits.
- Premature optimization: tuning a thing whose underlying premise is not yet validated.
- Anything that violates a hard rule and should simply stop.

A good optimizer pass usually cuts as much as it adds.
